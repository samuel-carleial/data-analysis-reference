(1) What is the independent variable? What is the dependent variable?

Here, we have an experiment set with two treatments (congruent and incongruent), in which the researcher estimated the time a participant took to distinguish the color of words. We have the dependent (or response) variable as the time of response by the participants. The independent variable is the one clearly established by the researcher in order for her/him to possibly explain the response variable. That is, the independent (explanatory) variable are the treatments Congruent and Incongruent.

(2) What is an appropriate set of hypotheses for this task? Specify your null and alternative hypotheses, and clearly define any notation used. Justify your choices.

In my opinion, the perception of color by our brains is supported by different visual cues, and when someone reads a word with the exact reference to its font color, the brain is somehow helped and therefore the response time should be faster than if it just "sees" the color without any other reference to it. Consequently, when the color is dismatched with the name, I would expect the brain to be induced to a confusion and error.

Therefore, the null hypothesis would be the scenario where we do not find significant differences between both treatments (variables Congruent and Incongruent). More specifically, since we expect that the Congruent treatment would produce lower times, we can say that the null hypothesis is the case where Congruent and Incongruent are equal or Congruent might even be larger than Incongruent, whereas the alternative hypothesis is the scenario where Congruent is lower than Incongruent.

Hence, we have the hypothesis below: \begin{equation} (H_{0}) \text{ Null hypothesis}: \mu_{c} - \mu_{i} >= 0 \end{equation}

\begin{equation} (H_{1}) \text{ Alternative hypothesis}: \mu_{c} - \mu_{i} < 0 \end{equation}

where $\mu_{c}$ is the population mean time needed to answer when color and word correctly match (Congruent), and $\mu_{i}$ is the mean population time needed to answer when color and word do not match (Incongruent).

To compare these two samples, we should use a paired T-test, given that sample size is very small and the results come from the same participants (they took both treatments, and therefore results are not entirely independent). Moreover, we want to test whether Congruent is indeed lower than Incongruent (one-sided).

According to my websearch, the assumptions for a T-test are:

  • The scale of measurement applied to the data collected follows a continuous or ordinal scale.
  • The data is collected from a representative, randomly selected portion of the total population.
  • The data, when plotted, results in a normal distribution, bell-shaped distribution curve.
  • A reasonably large sample size is used. A larger sample size means the distribution of results should approach a normal bell-shaped curve.
  • Homogeneity of variance. Homogeneous, or equal, variance exists when the standard deviations of samples are approximately equal.

The data is clearly continuous and I assume that it was well collected, because it was performed by a serious research group. Thus, the first two assumptions seem to hold. The data is not extremely large, but it is over 20 observations, so assumption four may also be respected (reasonable sample size). My calculations do not show that variances are exactly the same, but standard deviations are relatively close between of ca. 3 and 5. This leaves us with the third assumption still to be checked. Let's test the assumptions of a parametric data (normality), so that we know if we can use a normal T-test

If we observe the Q-Qplot above we see that the data fits to a normal distribution (red line), although some data still fall outside the fit. Given that this data is not large, it is naturally hard to find a well normally distributed data anyway.

In summary, we may say that the assumptions for a T-test are all, although not perfectly, respected. That supports us to follow on in the analysis.

First, I checked the assumptions a the T-test. I did not find strong evidence that the assumptions were broken, so I conducted the test using a t-test function in the scipy module for paired data (samples that are related). The t-test for a one-sided hypothesis resulted in a t-statistic of 8.02 and a p-value of < 0.001 (confidence level of 99%).

With this, we can reject the null hypothesis and accept the idea that time response for "Congruent" is indeed lower than "Incongruent". The result match perfectly with my expectations and visual representations of the data described previously.
