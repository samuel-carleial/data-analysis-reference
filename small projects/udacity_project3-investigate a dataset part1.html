Introduction

This project analyzes the dataset named "Medical Appointment No Shows". This dataset collects information from over 100k medical appointments in Brazil and is focused on the question of whether or not patients show up for their appointment. A number of characteristics about the patient are included in each row. The source of this data can be found at: Kaggle.

One person schedules an appointment, but still does not show up. Can we understand why this happens? (1) Do no-shows occur more often in specific days or months. (2) Do no-shows occur more often in men or women, or (3) do they relate with a person's health status? Finally, (4) is social support influecing the rate of appointment no-shows?

These four questions will be investigated below with a variety of descriptive statistics and graphical representations. For this, first I will load, inspect and modify the dataset in order to clean it and prepare it for further analysis. Then I will briefly describe the nature of its variables. Further, I will calculate various descriptive measures to look at the differences between groups of interest. Ultimately, I will produce some graphical representations to answer the questions posed here. I will end my analysis with a final section summing up my conclusions on this analysis.

Data description:

The data is composed of 14 variables (columns) and 110527 observations (rows).

"PatientId" and "AppointmentID" are set as numerical classes (i.e., float and int), but probably they should be analyzed as categorical variables (strings). Variables related to date ("ScheduleDay" and "AppointmentDay") are set as strings, but they should be set to datetime, in order for analysis to account for the specific time-points, not only weekdays, but maybe also months and years. "Scholarship" is of integer type ("0/1", which actually represents "no/yes"). The same is true for "Hipertension", "Diabetes", "Alcoholism", "Handcap" and "SMS_received". In order to facilitate the analysis, these variables should be edited accordingly.

However, not all variables would be interesting to use in the analysis. For example "AppointmentID" might not be informative to relate to "No-show", or the time an appointment was set, i.e., "ScheduleDay". These variables could be removed from the dataset, to spare some computing time and memory usage during the analysis.

Variable names could be edited, for better comprehension and ease of coding. "No-show" may be a tricky variable, and perhaps the opposite may be more easy for understanding the analysis. That is, whether persons did indeed go to the appointments ("attendance").

Finally, no missing values are present in this dataset. There might be duplicated rows in the data, thus it needs to be further checked and resolved.

Conclusions

Data suggests that patient attendance to consultations in Brazil might be related to some socio-demographic aspects of patients.

Concerning date, we cannot observe a strong pattern of month or weekday on appointment attendance. Suggesting that attendance to appointments is not related to specific months or days of the week. On Saturdays, there was a high variation in the proportion of attendances, nevertheless. This might be explained by the fact that on weekends, people are more inclined not to go to the doctor, producing more no-shows on this specific weekday.

In addition, when considering gender, men and women had similar attendance rates (80.27% and 80.4%, respectively). In other words, no-shows were approximately 20% of the total number of appointments scheduled. Men were in average younger than women (33+/-25 years old compared to 39+/-23 years old; mean +/- std), and from all successful appointments, men patients constituted only 35% compared to 65% of women.

Health conditions, namely hipertension, diabetes, alcoholism and handcap, do not seem to influence the rate of attendances (and consequently no-shows), since bar plots above show very similar values for the absence (blue bars) and presence (green bars) of the respective health conditions.

When looking at the social support (if patients had scholarships), actually there seems to be a slight negative trend related to attendance. I.e., patients with scholarships came relatively fewer times to appointments compared to patients without scholarships. This difference was about 5.2% (rate of patients with scholarship was 0.77 against 0.81 on the other group). This difference is not so striking. No-show rate for scholarship holders was 0.23 (1-0.77), whereas for patients without social support was in average 0.19 (1-0.81).

Finally, it is still important to bare in mind that these analysis are descriptive and no statistical tests have been carried out. In other words, we cannot truly explain the behavior of patients regarding their attendance to medical consultations by only looking at these few variables represented in some graphical visualizations. Statistical tests should also be performed, perhaps including additional variables that were not covered here in order for us to make stronger interpretations about this dataset.
